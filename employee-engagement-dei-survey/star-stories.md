## STAR Story

**Situation**: I undertook an HR analytics project to understand the true state of employee engagement at an organization — not just surface-level satisfaction, but the specific drivers that most influence whether employees stay, grow, and advocate for the company.

**Task**: Design a comprehensive engagement and DEI survey, administer it, segment the results, and build a prioritized action plan with measurable targets.

**Action**:
- Designed a 4-section survey covering eNPS, engagement drivers, manager effectiveness, and DEI — 20+ questions with 5-point Likert scale and open-ended items
- Administered to 50+ respondents via Google Forms with guaranteed anonymity
- Analyzed results: eNPS = 0; recognition (2.6/5) and growth (2.7/5) as lowest-scoring drivers
- Segmented findings by tenure and role; found new employees (eNPS: -12) and ICs (eNPS: -8) as highest-risk groups
- Applied driver analysis: identified which engagement items have strongest correlation with overall eNPS
- Built 4-priority action plan with specific interventions, targets, and 90-day pulse survey measurement plan

**Result**: A complete engagement diagnostic with eNPS benchmark, 6 key insight findings, segmented at-risk groups, and 4 prioritized, budget-conscious interventions targeting eNPS improvement from 0 to +20 in 12 months.

## HR Interview Q&A

**Q: How did you ensure survey responses were honest and not biased toward positive answers?**

"Three design choices. First, complete anonymity — no names, no employee IDs, no questions that could identify a respondent. Second, I used behavioral anchors rather than opinion questions where possible — 'My manager gives me feedback at least once a month' is harder to bias than 'My manager is good.' Third, I used eNPS as a calibration anchor — it's a globally benchmarked question with a numerical output that's hard to fake at the aggregate level. If the Likert scores are all 4s but eNPS is 0, there's a social desirability bias to investigate."

**Q: eNPS of 0 — is that bad? How would you present this to leadership?**

"I'd be honest but constructive. An eNPS of 0 is not a crisis — it means the company isn't actively damaging employee experience. But the +20 to +40 benchmark for high-engagement organizations sets the aspiration. The more important story is in the drivers: recognition at 2.6 and growth at 2.7 are the two most actionable levers, and they're low-cost. I'd present it as: 'We have a clear engagement opportunity, we know exactly where it is, and the top two fixes require behavior change from managers, not budget.' That's a much better frame than 'employees are disengaged.'"

**Q: How would you handle open-ended DEI responses that mention specific teams or managers?**

"Carefully. Open-ended responses are the richest data in an engagement survey, but they also carry the highest sensitivity risk. I'd aggregate and anonymize before presenting — never share a verbatim quote that could identify a respondent. For patterns that point to a specific team (e.g., 'women in technical roles need mentorship'), I'd flag it as a systemic finding, not a people finding, and engage the relevant department head in a solutions-focused conversation. The goal is improvement, not blame."
